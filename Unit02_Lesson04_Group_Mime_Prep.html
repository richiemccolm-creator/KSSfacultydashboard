Emotion Tennis
1
In pairs. Pass an imaginary tennis ball back and forth.
2
Each time you return it — change the emotional tone completely. Angry serve. Terrified return. Delighted lob. Disgusted smash.
3
Apply S.P.E.C.S. — slow the catch, exaggerate the reaction, keep each emotion clear and precise.
4
This warms up your body and your ability to switch between emotions instantly — both of which you need for your scene today.

Choose Your Scenario
Waiting for a Bus That Never Comes
Characters, relationships, the passing of time. What happens to the group as frustration grows? Who has highest status — and does it shift?
A Heist That Goes Wrong
Planning, sneaking, a mistake, a chase. Clear status between characters throughout. The complication must be visible — and physical.
A Day at the Beach in Terrible Weather
Setting up, enjoying it — then the weather turns. React to the environment physically. The setting is the complication.
Your scene must have a clear beginning (establish the world), a complication (something disrupts it), and a resolution (how does it end?). Every character must have a distinct status.

STRUCTURE·Every great mime scene has this shapeLesson 4
Your Scene Must Have This Shape
1
THE OPENING
Establish the world. Who are we? Where are we? What is the situation? The audience must understand this in the first 10 seconds — without a single word.
2
THE COMPLICATION
Something disrupts the situation. A problem, a surprise, a conflict. This is where the drama lives. It must be visible and physical — not just a change of expression.
3
THE RESOLUTION
How does it end? The scene must conclude — not just stop. The final image should tell the audience everything about how the situation was resolved.
Your scene runs for 60–90 seconds. Plan it carefully — 90 seconds disappears faster than you think.
BEFORE YOU START DEVISING — AGREE ON THESE THREE THINGS
What is the opening image? What is the complication? What is the final image?
