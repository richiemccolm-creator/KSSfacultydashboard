1800s
Melodrama was the most popular form of theatre in Victorian Britain. Audiences would shout, cheer, boo and throw things at the stage. The louder and more over-the-top — the better.
DISCUSS WITH YOUR PARTNER
Can you think of any modern films or TV shows that use the same idea — a clear villain, a clear hero, and an obvious moral? What makes them work?
Why do you think audiences loved to boo the villain and cheer the hero? What did it give them that other types of theatre didn't?
If you were going to melodrama tonight — how would you behave in the audience? What would you do differently from a normal show?

Melodrama Freeze Tag
1
One person is "it." When they tag you — freeze in an exaggerated melodrama pose immediately.
2
You can be freed by someone else striking a contrasting pose next to you — villain vs hero, for example.

Every freeze must be Exaggerated, Clear and held Slow — just like S.P.E.C.S. The audience must read you in one glance.

Stock Characters
THE VILLAIN
Manipulative, sneering, dark. Hunched shoulders, sidelong glances, wringing hands. The audience must boo the moment you appear.
Develop: a signature walk, a catchphrase gesture, and a memorable entrance.
THE HERO
Noble, brave, honest. Upright, chin raised, open gestures. Always high status. The audience must cheer the moment you appear.
Develop: a signature walk, a heroic gesture, and a dramatic entrance.
THE VICTIM
Innocent, emotional, helpless. Wide eyes, clasped hands, shrinks away. Creates sympathy from the audience immediately.
Develop: a signature walk, a distress gesture, and a pitiful entrance.
THE SIDEKICK
Bumbling, clumsy, physical comedy. Lower status. Their misfortune makes the audience laugh — and relieves the tension.
Develop: a clumsy walk, a comic gesture, and a chaotic entrance.
The audience must know who you are in 5 seconds. Your walk, your first gesture, your entrance — these are your introduction. Make them unmistakable.

Boo & Cheer
👎
BOO!
For the villain. As loud and dramatic as you can. The villain feeds off your reaction.
👏
CHEER!
For the hero. The louder you cheer — the more the hero rises. You power the performance.
😱
GASP!
For the twist or the peril. A collective gasp from the audience tells the performer they got it right.
1
Performers — play a short exchange between villain and hero. Make every moment Exaggerated, Clear and Slow.
2
Audience — react fully. Boo, cheer, gasp at the right moments. Your reaction is part of the performance.
3
Performers — notice how the audience reaction changes your energy. Let it fuel the performance.
DISCUSS AFTER
How did the audience reaction change the performance? Which S.P.E.C.S. elements became more important when the audience was responding?
