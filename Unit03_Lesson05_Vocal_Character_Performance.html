Vocal Character Cards
The Nervous Teenager
Pace: Fast — thoughts tumble out before they are ready
Pitch: High — rises further under pressure
Volume: Quiet at the start, louder when frustrated
Projection: Directed downward — avoids eye contact
Intonation: Rising at the end of every sentence
Register: Informal — slang, contractions, half-finished thoughts
The Cold Authority
Pace: Slow and measured — every word deliberate
Pitch: Very low — no variation. Controlled at all times.
Volume: Quiet. They never need to raise their voice.
Projection: Perfect — reaches everywhere without effort
Intonation: Flat. Statements only. No questions.
Register: Formal. Precise. No wasted words.
The Desperate Optimist
Pace: Varies — slow when persuading, fast when afraid
Pitch: Mid to high — brightness covering fear
Volume: Bright and full — performing cheerfulness
Projection: Strong — filling the room to fill the silence
Intonation: Rises constantly — seeking agreement
Register: Warm, informal — desperate to connect
The Controlled Rage
Pace: Dangerously slow — the anger makes them careful
Pitch: Drops lower with every sentence
Volume: Starts quiet. The audience leans in. It does not rise.
Projection: Laser-focused — aimed directly at one person
Intonation: Flat but with sudden sharp drops on key words
Register: Formal — control is the weapon, not volume
You may use one of these profiles or create your own. Either way — plan every element before you speak a word. Vocal choices that aren't planned are accidents, not performance.

Monologue Performance
1
Perform your character monologue — 60 seconds minimum. Apply all six P.P.V.P.I.R. elements throughout.
2
If you feel yourself dropping back into your own voice — slow down. Pace is always the reset button.
3
Your partner watches and listens. They will give you feedback using the frame on the next slide.
Pace — is it consistent with the character throughout?
Pitch — does it stay in the character's range, or drift into the performer's own voice?
Volume and projection — can you hear every word clearly from where you are sitting?
Intonation — does the rise and fall match the character's emotional state?
Register — does the way they speak reveal who they are and how they relate to the listener?
AFTER THE PERFORMANCE
Which P.P.V.P.I.R. element was hardest to sustain throughout — and at what point did you feel it starting to slip?

PEER FEEDBACK·Specific, vocabulary-driven, constructiveAfter performance
P.P.V.P.I.R. Feedback Frame
Use these sentence starters. Name the element, name the moment, name the effect.
"The P.P.V.P.I.R. element that was strongest was… because…"
Name a specific moment. What did you hear that told you that element was working?
"The moment that communicated character most clearly was… because the [element] showed…"
Point to one specific moment. What specifically did you hear — and what did it tell you about the character?
"The P.P.V.P.I.R. element that could be developed further is… because…"
Be constructive. What would it sound like if that element were stronger — describe it precisely.
"The point where the character felt most real was… and the point where I felt the performer's own voice coming through was…"
Honesty helps. Naming the moment where it slipped is the most useful feedback you can give.
REMEMBER
Good feedback uses vocabulary, names moments, and explains effect. "It was good" helps no one. "Your pitch dropped into low register when you said the final line and it made the character sound genuinely threatening" — that helps everyone.
