Guidance: Writing effective LIs and SCs Show guidance ▼

What makes a good Learning Intention

Focus on learning, not activity. Use the stem "We are learning to..." and complete with a clear outcome. One or two linked ideas per lesson. Avoid "We are doing..." or "We are learning about..." for skills.

What makes a good Success Criterion

Use the "I can..." format. Each SC should be observable—something you can see or hear. Avoid "I understand" or "I know." Keep SCs achievable within the lesson. Aim for 3–5 per lesson.

Worked example (Drama):
LI: We are learning to use proxemics to show relationships between characters.
SCs: I can position myself on stage to show status and distance; I can use levels to show power; I can explain my staging choices.

Common pitfalls to avoid

  • Too many SCs (more than 5 becomes a checklist nobody uses)
  • Mixing activity with learning ("I can complete the worksheet" vs "I can identify three elements")
  • Vague language ("I understand", "I know")
  • Jargon without ensuring pupils know the meaning

EXA Es+Os quick reference

Drama: EXA 3-12a — Creating and presenting drama. Art: EXA 3-02a, 3-03a — Responding and creating. Photography: Same EXA framework. Align your SCs with what the outcome expects.

    Quick Reference: Restorative Questions
    • What happened? — Allow each person to tell their version. Use first when opening the conversation.
    • What were you thinking at the time? — Explores motivation without blame. Follow-up: "What did you need?"
    • Who has been affected? — Acknowledges impact on others. Helps pupils see beyond themselves.
    • What needs to happen to put things right? — Agree next steps together. Finish with concrete actions.

    Affective statement example: "I felt disappointed when the rehearsal stopped—I know you can work through this." Shows your feelings without blame.

    Quick Reference: Feedback Principles

    Effective feedback is specific, timely and actionable. Link it to success criteria so pupils know what to improve. Focus on one or two things—more overwhelms. Build in pupil response: ask them to say what they will do next, or to apply the feedback before you move on. Weak: "Good work." Strong: "You have used proxemics to show status—in your next scene, try varying the distance when the power shifts."

    Quick Reference: Retrieval Techniques

    Use 3–5 minutes at the start or mid-lesson. Keep it low-stakes—no grading. Concrete prompts: "Write three things that affect depth of field"; "List the steps for developing a print"; "What makes a strong composition?" Use Do Now starters, mini-whiteboards, pair-share, or quick brain dumps. Crucially, link retrieval to application: after pupils recall, have them use it (e.g. "Now apply that in your next five shots").

    Best Practice Examples

    Drama — Restorative circle after group conflict

    A S3 devising group fell out over role allocation. One pupil felt repeatedly overlooked for the main part; another accused the group of not listening. The teacher ran a short restorative circle: each person shared what happened and how they were affected, using a talking object. The focus was on impact ("I felt left out") and what was needed ("I need a chance to have my ideas heard"). They agreed new norms: "We will listen when someone suggests an idea"; "We will take turns to lead a section." The teacher wrote these down and checked in a week later. The group repaired, completed the performance, and reflected that the circle had helped them work better together.

    Art — Feed-forward from one piece to the next

    After reviewing a pupil's expressive painting, the teacher noticed that tone was applied in flat patches rather than built gradually. Instead of correcting every area, the teacher gave one feed-forward focus: "In your next piece, build tone gradually in layers—lighter first, then darken. Annotate your sketchbook with this intention before you start." The pupil did so, and in the next piece the improvement was clear. The feed-forward was more useful than a long list of corrections on the previous work, because it gave the pupil a clear target for the next task and the chance to apply it while the learning was fresh.

    Photography — Retrieval then application

    The teacher started the lesson with: "Write three things that affect depth of field." Two minutes. Then pair-share so pupils could add to each other's lists. The teacher did not collect or grade—this was low-stakes. Next: "Use depth of field deliberately in your next five shots. Choose whether you want shallow or deep for each, and say why." The retrieval strengthened recall before pupils applied it in the shoot. Pupils reported that having just recalled the concepts made them more intentional when composing. The teacher observed more deliberate use of aperture choices.

    LI/SC — Sharing and revisiting

    LIs and SCs were on the board at the start: "We are learning to use proxemics to show relationships." Three success criteria. The teacher read them aloud and asked a pupil to put the LI in their own words. During rehearsal, when circulating, the teacher asked: "Which success criterion are you working on?" and "How will you show me you have met it?" In the plenary: "Thumbs up if you have met the first criterion; thumbs sideways if you are still working on it." The teacher then invited two pupils to demonstrate. The learning journey was visible from the first minute to the last, and pupils could articulate what they had learned.
