Pitch Spectrum
HIGH PITCH
High pitch tends to communicate excitement, fear, youth, nervousness or a lack of control. It can make a character seem less powerful — or more emotionally exposed.
Examples: a frightened child, someone receiving shocking news, an excited announcement, a character losing control of a situation.
LOW PITCH
Low pitch tends to communicate authority, age, calm, threat or gravitas. It makes a character seem more in control — or more dangerous. The audience leans in to hear it.
Examples: a villain making a threat, a leader giving a command, someone hiding enormous emotion, a character holding a secret.
1
Perform the same sentence from Lesson 1 — first at your highest comfortable pitch, then at your lowest.
2
Now link it to Unit 02 status work: does high status always mean low pitch? Test this with your partner and be ready to argue your answer.
BIG QUESTION
Can a high-pitched voice ever communicate high status — and if so, how?

Voice Only Scene
Choose one scenario. Perform a 30-second exchange — no movement at all. Pace and pitch must do everything.
The Phone Call
One person delivers bad news. The other receives it. No words tell us what the news is — only the voice reveals the truth.
The Interrogation
One character has power. One does not. The power shifts halfway through. Show this entirely through how pace and pitch change.
The Apology
One character apologises. Do they mean it? The audience must decide — from the pace and pitch alone, not the words.
1
Plan your vocal choices before you perform — agree where pace speeds up, where pitch drops, where you pause.
2
Audience: identify the emotional shift in the scene and name which P.P.V.P.I.R. element caused it.
