Volume vs Projection
SHOUTING (volume only)
Forces air out using throat tension
Damages the voice over time
Sounds aggressive, not powerful
The audience pulls back from it
Breaks clarity — words blur together
Tells the audience you've lost control
PROJECTING (supported voice)
Uses breath and resonance — not throat
Sustainable across a full performance
Sounds authoritative and controlled
The audience leans in to receive it
Preserves clarity — every word lands
Tells the audience you are completely in control
The rule: Projection comes from your breath, resonates in your chest and skull, and is directed at a specific point — like a beam of light aimed at the back wall. Volume is just turning up the dial. They are not the same thing.

The Projection Challenge
1
Stand 1 metre from your partner. Speak the sentence clearly. Now move to 3 metres apart. Then 5 metres. Then opposite sides of the room.
2
The rule: your volume must not increase as the distance grows. Only your projection and breath support can change.
3
Partner listening: can you hear every single word clearly? Is it the volume or the direction and support of the voice that carries it to you?
4
Now try: whisper with full projection. Direct it precisely. Does a fully supported whisper carry further than a shout?
DISCUSS AFTER
What physically changed in your body when you projected further — and what does that tell you about where projection actually comes from?
Stand tall, breathe from the diaphragm, direct the voice at a specific point on the back wall. Think of projection like a torch beam — you aim it, not amplify it.

Volume Ladder
Perform your monologue text with three deliberate volume changes. The audience must map the emotional arc from the volume alone.
