WARM UP· Get your voice ready — every lesson starts here 0–8 min
0–8

Vocal Warm-Up
💨
BREATH
Slow breath in for 4 counts. Hold for 4. Out for 8. Repeat three times. Your voice lives in your breath.
🎵
HUM
Hum on one note. Feel the vibration in your lips, chest, skull. Move the hum up and down a scale slowly.
🌊
SIREN
Say "weeee-ooooo" sliding from your highest pitch to your lowest and back. Loosen the voice — no tension.
👄
TONGUE TWISTER
"Red lorry, yellow lorry" — start slow, build speed. Clarity first, pace second. Never sacrifice one for the other.
1

THE FRAMEWORK· Learn this — you will use it every lesson this unit 8–20 min
P.P.V.P.I.R.
Six elements of vocal performance — every voice choice you make in Drama uses at least one of these
P
PACE
How fast or slow you speak. Urgency, tension, confidence — pace controls all of them.
P
PITCH
How high or low your voice is. High pitch can show excitement or fear. Low pitch suggests authority or age.
V
VOLUME
How loud or quiet. Volume variation creates dramatic effect — silence can be as powerful as a shout.
P
PROJECTION
The strength and direction of your voice so everyone can hear — even with your back turned. Not the same as loud.
I
INTONATION
The rising and falling of your voice in speech. Intonation carries meaning — the same words can mean opposite things.
R
REGISTER
Whether your speech is formal or informal. Register reveals relationship, status and character instantly.
QUICK CHECK — WITHOUT LOOKING
Cover the cards and tell your partner what all six letters stand for. If you get stuck — that's the one to remember first.

ACTIVITY· One sentence — six completely different meanings 20–38 min
20–38
minutes
The Same Sentence
YOUR SENTENCE:
"I didn't say you took the money."
Seven words. Infinite meanings. It all depends on which word you emphasise — and how.
Change PACE
Speak it very slowly, then very fast. How does urgency or calm change what you think the speaker means?
Which pace makes the speaker sound more guilty?
Change PITCH
Speak it in a very high pitch, then a very low pitch. Which sounds more threatening? Which sounds more nervous?
What does pitch tell us about the character's status?
Change VOLUME
Start at a whisper. End at full volume. Or start loud and drop to almost nothing. What does each choice communicate?
Which volume makes the audience lean in furthest?
Change INTONATION
Stress a different word each time — I / say / you / took / the / money. Six completely different meanings from one sentence.
Which word changes the meaning the most dramatically?
Change REGISTER
Say it in a very formal register — then in a casual, informal way. How does the character's relationship to the listener change?
Which register makes it feel more dangerous?
Change PROJECTION
Speak it to someone next to you — then project it to the back of the room. What physically changes in your body to make that happen?
What does projection feel like in your chest and throat?

Partner Listening
1
In pairs. Person A speaks the sentence — applying one P.P.V.P.I.R. element as strongly as possible. Do not tell your partner which one.
2
Person B listens. Then names: which element was strongest — and what effect it created on them as the listener.
3
Swap. Repeat. Then try combining two elements at once — does it become harder or easier to identify them separately?
4
Challenge: can you apply all six elements in one single delivery of the sentence?
CLASS DISCUSSION AFTER
Which P.P.V.P.I.R. element was hardest to identify when listening — and which was hardest to control when performing?
This is exactly what an audience does every time you perform — they experience your vocal choices even if they can't name them. Your job is to make those choices deliberately, not accidentally.
