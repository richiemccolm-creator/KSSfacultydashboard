Choose Your Scenario
The Villain Tries to Steal the Orphanage
A classic melodrama setup. The villain has a dastardly plan. The hero must stop them. The victim is helpless. The sidekick makes everything worse — then accidentally saves the day.
A Hero Must Save the Town
The villain has a scheme. The town is in peril. Only the hero can save everyone — but can they do it before it's too late? The sidekick helps. Badly. Then brilliantly.
A Case of Mistaken Identity
Someone is not who they appear to be. The confusion grows. The villain exploits it. The hero sorts it out — eventually. The sidekick causes the most chaos.
Your scene must include: all four stock characters · one moment of direct audience address · a clear resolution.

Devise & Rehearse
The audience knows who the villain is in the first 5 seconds — before a word is spoken
Every character has their own signature walk, gesture and entrance
There is at least one moment of direct audience address — a character steps out and speaks to us directly
The scene has a clear resolution — the audience knows how it ends and who wins
All five S.P.E.C.S. elements are applied — especially Exaggerated and Slow in the key moments
REHEARSAL STRATEGY
Run it once. Find the weakest moment. Fix it specifically. Run it again. Repeat.

KEY MOMENTS·Every great melodrama scene has these momentsLesson 7
Don't Forget These
1
The villain's reveal — the moment the audience first sees the villain do something villainous. Slow it down. Let the audience react.
2
The hero's moment of doubt — even the hero faces a moment when it seems impossible. This creates tension before the resolution.
3
The direct address — one character steps out of the scene and speaks directly to the audience. This must be Slow, Clear and completely Exaggerated.
4
The sidekick's contribution — they must either make things worse at a crucial moment, or accidentally save the day. Not both. One.
5
The final image — freeze. The audience needs one last, clear image that tells them it's over and who won. Make it unmistakable.
In Lesson 8 you perform this scene. Not a rehearsal — a performance. To a real audience. Commit to it completely.
